How to Practice Sudoku

Sudoku should be a gradual process, starting with fundamental techniques and progressing to more complex pattern recognition.

I. Beginner Stage

1. Scanning and Filling

  • Singles: The most basic technique. A cell is a Single if it is the only possible location for a specific digit within its row, column, or block.
  • Cross-Hatching: Use the numbers already filled to eliminate possibilities across rows and columns, pinpointing the correct position for a digit within a specific block.

2. Pencil Marking

Lightly mark (or use a helper feature to display) all possible candidate digits in every empty cell. This is the crucial step for advanced solving.

II. Intermediate Stage

1. Naked Candidates

  • Naked Pair: In the same row, column, or block, two cells contain only two identical candidates (e.g., {1, 5}). These two digits must occupy these two cells, allowing you to eliminate 1 and 5 from the candidates of all other cells in that area.
  • Naked Triple: Similarly, three cells share a combination of up to three candidate digits, allowing you to eliminate those digits from the candidates of the other cells in that area.

2. Hidden Candidates

  • Hidden Single: In a row, column, or block, if a candidate digit (e.g., 7) only appears in the candidate list of one cell, then that cell must be 7, even if it has other candidates.
  • Hidden Pair/Triple: In an area, if two (or three) digits appear only in the candidate lists of two (or three) cells, then all other candidates in those specific cells can be eliminated.

III. Advanced Stage

1. Wing Techniques

  • X-Wing: In two rows (or columns), the two candidate positions for a digit (e.g., 4) form a rectangle. This structure allows you to eliminate the candidate 4 from cells in the other columns (or rows) that align with the rectangle.
  • Swordfish / Jellyfish: Extensions of the X-Wing, involving patterns across three rows and three columns (Swordfish) or four rows and four columns (Jellyfish).

2. Chaining and Forcing Chains

XY-Chains: More complex logical chains involving multiple cells and two candidates, using logical inference to determine the true state of one endpoint, thus eliminating candidates elsewhere.
