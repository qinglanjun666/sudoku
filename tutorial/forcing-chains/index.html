Forcing Chains

1. What You Will Master

In this section, you will learn Forcing Chains. You will learn how to test multiple hypotheses simultaneously to find a "Logical Constant." You will discover that sometimes, no matter which path you take from a specific starting point, the destination is the same. This allows you to eliminate candidates that are impossible in all timelines.

2. Main Content

The Concept

Most techniques (like X-Wing or AIC) are Linear: "If A, then B, then C..." Forcing Chains are Branched: "If A, then C. BUT... If B, also C."

It addresses the question: "What if I don't know whether Cell X is a 1 or a 2?" The Method: You trace the consequences of both options. The Goal: You are looking for a Convergence.

"If I pick 1, cell Z becomes 5." "If I pick 2, cell Z also becomes 5." Conclusion: Cell Z MUST be 5. It is an unavoidable truth.

Types of Forcing Chains

Cell Forcing Chain (The "Bifurcation")

Start: A Bi-Value Cell with candidates {1, 2}. Path A: Assume the cell is 1. Follow the chain → Result: Target is OFF. Path B: Assume the cell is 2. Follow the chain → Result: Target is OFF. Result: Since the cell must be 1 or 2, the Target is OFF in all realities. Eliminate it.

Unit Forcing Chain (The "Region Check")

Start: A Row has '7' in only two spots (Strong Link). Path A: Assume Spot 1 is '7' → Result X. Path B: Assume Spot 2 is '7' → Result X. Result: Result X is confirmed.

Nishio Forcing Chain (The "Contradiction")

This is a single-branch forcing chain. "If I set this cell to '5', does the puzzle break immediately?" If yes → The cell is not '5'. Note: Many purists consider this "guessing" (Trial & Error), but if the logical steps are short enough to visualize without writing, it is considered a valid technique named after professional player Nishio.

The Controversy: Logic vs. Guessing

Forcing Chains are controversial. Pure Logic: "I can visualize both paths in my head simultaneously. I see the convergence." (Valid). Guessing (Bowman's Bingo): "I will write down a number in pen, solve for 20 minutes, see if I get stuck, then erase." (Invalid strategy).

To use Forcing Chains properly, you should generally keep the chains short. If you need to simulate 15 steps for each branch, you aren't solving with logic; you are just simulating the future.

How to Use It

Find a "Pivot Cell" (usually a Bi-Value Cell) that connects to dense clusters of clues. Ask: "Does this pair {A, B} exert pressure on a distant cell?" Mental Check: "If A... [chain]... Target is 4." Mental Check: "If B... [chain]... Target is 4." If they match, write the 4.

3. Section Summary

Forcing Chains are the "Quantum Mechanics" of Sudoku. You exist in super-position, exploring two parallel universes. When those universes agree on a single fact, that fact becomes reality. This is the ultimate tool in your arsenal—capable of solving any solvable puzzle, provided you have the memory and patience to trace the threads.
