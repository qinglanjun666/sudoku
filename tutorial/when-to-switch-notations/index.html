When to Switch Notations

1. What You Will Master

In this section, you will learn the tactical timing of switching from Snyder Notation to Full Notation. You will master the Hybrid Strategy: identifying specific areas (Houses) that are "dense" enough to benefit from full marks, without flooding the entire board. This keeps your puzzle-solving efficient and focused.

2. Main Content

The "Brick Wall" Moment

Every solver hits a moment where progress stops.

You have scanned 1–9.

You have marked all Snyder pairs.

You have cleaned up all obvious singles.

Result: You are stuck.

This is the signal to switch strategies. But you don't need to fill the whole board yet.

Targeted Filling (The Hybrid Approach)

Instead of auto-filling all 81 cells, look for Constrained Houses.

  • Identify: Find a Row, Column, or Box that has very few empty cells left (e.g., only 3 or 4 empty cells).
  • Fill Only There: Add full candidates only to those specific cells.
  • Analyze: Check if these new candidates create a Naked Pair or Triple (which we will learn in the next chapter).

The Transition Workflow

  • Phase 1: Pure Scanning (No marks).
  • Phase 2: Snyder Notation (Marking pairs).
  • Phase 3: Targeted Filling (Marking specific rows/cols with <5 empty cells).
  • Phase 4: Full Notation (The "Nuclear Option" — fill everything to find complex fish/chains).

Cleaning as You Go

Crucial Rule: Whenever you place a number (e.g., a big '5'), you must immediately erase the candidate '5's from all peers (Row, Col, Box).

If you don't clean up, your Full Notation becomes a "Lying Notation," and you will make logic errors.

3. Section Summary

The transition from Snyder to Full Notation should be gradual, not sudden. Start by fully marking only the most restricted areas of the board. This often reveals hidden subsets without creating visual chaos. Remember: Notation is a tool to help your memory, not a replacement for your eyes. Keep the board as clean as possible for as long as possible.
