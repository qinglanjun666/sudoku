Terminology: Cells, Houses, and Peers

1. What You Will Master

To think like a Sudoku master, you must speak the language. In this section, you will move beyond basic rows and columns to learn the professional terms: House, Peer, and Candidate. Understanding these definitions is crucial because all complex solving techniques are built upon the relationships between these elements.

2. Main Content

1. The House (Unit / Region)

The term House is a generic name for any group of 9 cells that must contain the digits 1–9. Since the rules apply equally to Rows, Columns, and Boxes, we call them all "Houses."

Every grid has 27 Houses in total: 9 Rows + 9 Columns + 9 Boxes.

Why it matters: When a technique works in a Row, it usually works in a Column or Box too. We say, "This logic applies to the House."

2. Peers (Buddies / Neighbors)

Two cells are Peers if they share the same House. If Cell A is a peer of Cell B, they cannot contain the same number. Any specific cell on the grid has exactly 20 Peers:

  • 8 cells in the same Row.
  • 8 cells in the same Column.
  • 4 remaining cells in the same Box.

3. Givens vs. Solved Cells

Givens (Clues): The numbers printed on the grid at the start of the puzzle. You cannot change them.

Solved Cells: Empty cells that you have correctly filled in with a number.

4. Candidates (Pencil Marks)

Candidates are the small notes you write inside an empty cell to indicate what numbers are possible. For example, if a cell is not "seen" by a 1 or a 2 in any of its houses, you might pencil in small "1" and "2" as candidates.

Note: In Level 1, we solve without writing candidates. In Level 2, we will learn how to use them strategically.

3. Section Summary

Vocabulary allows for precision. A House is any Row, Column, or Box. Peers are cells that live in the same House and "see" each other. Candidates are potential numbers for an empty cell. If a cell contains a '5', none of its 20 Peers can be a '5'. This simple relationship is the foundation of all exclusion logic.
