The Unique Solution Rule

1. What You Will Master

In this section, you will learn the controversial but incredibly powerful Unique Solution Rule. You will understand what a "Deadly Pattern" is—a specific configuration of four cells that would destroy the puzzle's validity. You will learn that your goal in this chapter is not to find a pattern, but to prevent one from forming.

2. Main Content

The Axiom

Most Sudoku apps and tournaments operate on a golden rule: "A valid Sudoku puzzle must have exactly one unique solution." If a puzzle has two different solutions, it is considered "broken" or invalid. We can use this assumption to eliminate candidates.

Note: If you are solving a handmade puzzle from a newspaper that might be flawed, this logic is risky. But for computer-generated or app-based puzzles, it is 100% safe.

The "Deadly Pattern" (2x2)

Imagine a rectangle of four cells:

  • Row 1: Cell A and Cell B.
  • Row 9: Cell C and Cell D.
  • They form a perfect rectangle (sharing rows and columns).
  • All four cells share the exact same two candidates, e.g., {2, 7}.
  • Crucially: These four cells are contained within exactly two Boxes. (e.g., A and C are in Box 1; B and D are in Box 2).

Why It Is Deadly

If this pattern exists (four cells, two boxes, candidates {2, 7}), the puzzle is broken.

Solution 1:
Top Left: 2, Top Right: 7
Bottom Left: 7, Bottom Right: 2

Solution 2:
Top Left: 7, Top Right: 2
Bottom Left: 2, Bottom Right: 7

Because these cells don't affect any other rows/cols/boxes differently (since 2 and 7 are switched locally within their own closed loop), both solutions would be valid. This means the puzzle has two answers. Since we know the puzzle is valid, this state is impossible.

The Strategy: Avoidance

You never look for a Deadly Pattern to keep it. You look for a pattern that is about to become deadly, and you kill it before it happens. "If I put a 7 here, it creates a Deadly Pattern. Therefore, I cannot put a 7 here."

The Conditions for a Deadly Pattern

  • Rectangular: 4 cells forming a rectangle.
  • Binary: All cells contain only candidates X and Y.
  • Two Boxes: The cells must sit in exactly two boxes (if they are spread across 4 boxes, the logic doesn't work).

3. Section Summary

The Unique Solution Rule acts as a "Guard Rail." It tells you where you cannot go. If a specific move would trap you in an interchangeable loop of digits, that move is illegal. In the next few sections, we will apply this to the "Unique Rectangle" (UR) techniques, which are the practical weapons derived from this rule.
