The Rules of Sudoku

1. What You Will Master

By the end of this section, you will understand the fundamental structure of the Sudoku grid and the three "Golden Rules" that govern every puzzle. You will learn to identify the basic units—Rows, Columns, and Boxes—and understand why a valid Sudoku puzzle must have exactly one unique solution.

2. Main Content

The Grid Structure

Standard Sudoku is played on a 9×9 grid, which consists of 81 individual squares called Cells. This large grid is further divided into nine smaller 3×3 sub-grids, which are commonly referred to as Boxes (or sometimes "Blocks" or "Regions"). At the start of the game, some cells are pre-filled with numbers. These are known as "givens" or "clues."

The Objective

The goal is simple: fill every empty cell with an integer from 1 to 9. However, you cannot place numbers randomly. You must adhere to three strict constraints.

The Three Golden Rules

  • The Row Rule: Every horizontal Row must contain the numbers 1 through 9 exactly once. No number can be repeated in the same row.
  • The Column Rule: Every vertical Column must contain the numbers 1 through 9 exactly once. No number can be repeated in the same column.
  • The Box Rule: Every 3×3 Box must contain the numbers 1 through 9 exactly once. No number can be repeated within the same box.

Logic, Not Math

A common misconception is that Sudoku requires arithmetic. It does not. You never add the numbers up to 45. The numbers 1–9 are merely symbols; the game could technically be played with 9 different letters (A–I) or 9 different colors. Sudoku is a game of pure logic and elimination.

3. Section Summary

Sudoku is a puzzle of placement based on constraints. Remember the mantra: "One to Nine, No Repeats." If you place a number that duplicates another in the same Row, Column, or Box, the move is invalid. Every valid Sudoku puzzle implies a single, unique solution that can be reached without guessing.
