Simple Coloring (Single's Chain)

1. What You Will Master

In this section, you will learn Simple Coloring. You will stop looking for rectangles and start looking for "Chains." You will learn how to trace a single number across the board using an "ON / OFF" logic. This technique allows you to detect contradictions between cells that are very far apart, proving that certain candidates are impossible.

2. Main Content

The Building Block: The Conjugate Pair

Before you can chain, you must understand the Conjugate Pair (often called a "Strong Link").

A Conjugate Pair exists when a House (Row, Col, or Box) has exactly two cells that contain a specific candidate.

The Logic: Since the number must be in one of them, if one is ON (True), the other must be OFF (False). Conversely, if one is OFF, the other is ON.

The Chain

"Simple Coloring" is just connecting these pairs together.

Pick a Number: Let's track the number '2'.

Start: Find a Conjugate Pair in Row 1. Mark one cell "Color A" (Blue) and the other "Color B" (Green).

Extend: Does the Green cell connect to another Conjugate Pair in Column 3?

If yes, the Green cell forces the next cell to be Blue.

Repeat: Continue labeling A-B-A-B-A-B across the grid.

The Trap: How to Eliminate

Once you have colored the chain, look for two specific flaws:

Rule 1: Two Colors Seeing the Same Cell

Look for an uncolored cell that contains a '2'.

Can this cell "see" (intersect with) a Blue cell AND a Green cell?

Logic: Since one color must be True and the other False, one of those two colored cells will be a '2'. Therefore, the uncolored neighbor cannot be a '2'.

Action: Eliminate the candidate from that uncolored cell.

Rule 2: Two Colors in the Same House

Did your chain force two cells of the same color (e.g., two Blues) to land in the same Row, Column, or Box?

Logic: A house cannot have two '2's. Therefore, the Blue color must be False.

Action: All Blue cells are false (delete the '2's). All Green cells are True (fill in the '2's).

Why "Simple" Coloring?

It is called "Simple" because it only uses Strong Links (houses with exactly 2 candidates). Later, in Level 4 (Expert), you will learn chains that use "Weak Links" (houses with 3+ candidates), which is much harder.

3. Section Summary

Simple Coloring turns Sudoku into a binary switch. By tracing the relationship of "If this, then that," you create a web of logic. If the web ends up pointing two loaded guns at a single cell (Rule 1), that cell is dead. If the web tangles itself up (Rule 2), the whole color is dead.
