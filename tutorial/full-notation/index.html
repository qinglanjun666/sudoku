Full Notation (The Candidate List)

1. What You Will Master

In this section, you will learn Full Notation (also known as Auto-Fill or Full Candidate List). While Snyder Notation is great for speed, there comes a point in difficult puzzles where you simply run out of "easy" moves. You will master the skill of correctly listing every possible candidate for a cell, preparing the ground for advanced elimination techniques.

2. Main Content

When to Use Full Notation

You should switch to Full Notation only when:

  • Snyder has stalled: You have scanned all numbers 1–9, marked all pairs, and still cannot find a single digit to place.
  • The puzzle is "Hard" or "Expert": Simple geometry is no longer enough; you need to find logical contradictions within the candidates.

How to Fill Correctly

To fill candidates manually without making mistakes:

  • Focus on one cell at a time.
  • Count 1 to 9: Check the Row, Column, and Box for the number '1'. If not present, write '1'. Check for '2'. If present, skip. Repeat until 9. Move to the next cell.

Note: Many modern Sudoku apps (and your website) have an "Auto-Candidates" button. It is okay to use this, but understanding how to generate it manually ensures you understand the logic.

The Danger of Full Notation

  • Visual Noise: The grid becomes incredibly cluttered. It is easy to miss simple patterns like "Hidden Singles" because they are buried under a mountain of tiny numbers.
  • The Trap: Novice players often turn on Full Notation too early. This makes them lazy scanners. Always try to solve as much as possible with Snyder Notation before flooding the board.

Reading the List

Once Full Notation is active, your goal shifts. You are no longer looking for "Where can the 7 go?" (Placement). You are now looking for "Which candidates can I remove?" (Elimination).

If a cell has candidates {1, 5, 9} and you find logic that proves it can't be 1 or 5...

...then it must be 9. This is a Naked Single emerging from the noise.

3. Section Summary

Full Notation is a powerful tool, but it must be used wisely. It reveals the complete state of the board, showing every possible option for every cell. This visibility is required for advanced strategies like Naked Pairs and X-Wings. However, turning it on too early can hinder your ability to see simple geometric solutions. Use it as a last resort when the easy logic runs dry.
