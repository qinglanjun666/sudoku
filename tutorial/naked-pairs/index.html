Naked Pairs

1. What You Will Master

In this section, you will learn to spot Naked Pairs. This technique allows you to eliminate candidates even when you don't know exactly which number goes where. You will understand the logic of "Locked Sets": how two cells acting together can dominate a row, column, or box, clearing the way for other solutions.

2. Main Content

The Concept

A Naked Pair occurs when any two cells in the same House (Row, Column, or Box) contain exactly the same two candidates—and only those two candidates.

Example: In Row 3, you find two cells that both contain the candidates [2, 7].

Cell A: 2, 7

Cell B: 2, 7

The Logic: "The Lock"

Even though we don't know if Cell A is 2 or 7, we know for a fact that Cell A and Cell B are a closed loop.

If Cell A is 2, then Cell B must be 7.

If Cell A is 7, then Cell B must be 2.

In either scenario, the numbers 2 and 7 are completely used up by these two cells. Therefore, no other cell in that same House can contain a 2 or a 7.

How to Use It

  • Spot the Pair: Look for two cells in a group that look identical (e.g., both say 4, 8).
  • Check the House: Verify they are in the same Row, Column, or Box.
  • Eliminate: Look at all other cells in that same House. If you see a small pencil mark for 4 or 8 in those other cells, erase them.
  • Solve: Often, erasing these candidates will leave behind a Naked Single in a different cell.

Common Mistake

Do not confuse a Naked Pair with a loose pair.

Cell A: 2, 7

Cell B: 2, 7, 9 (This is NOT a Naked Pair because of the 9).

For a Naked Pair to work, both cells must be "Naked"—meaning they have no other extra candidates.

3. Section Summary

A Naked Pair is a "Keep Out" sign for the rest of the House. When you find two cells locked with the same two numbers (e.g., A/B and A/B), those numbers belong exclusively to them. You can safely wipe those candidates from the rest of the row, column, or box. This clearing process often reveals the next step of the puzzle.
