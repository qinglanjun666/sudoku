Jellyfish

1. What You Will Master

In this section, you will learn the Jellyfish. This is the 4×4 extension of the X-Wing and Swordfish. You will understand how to identify four rows (or columns) that coordinate to lock a specific candidate into four perpendicular lines, allowing you to clear massive amounts of candidates from the rest of the grid.

2. Main Content

The Concept

A Jellyfish occurs when a specific digit appears in exactly four Rows, and within those rows, the candidate falls into exactly four Columns.

Base Set: 4 Rows.

Cover Set: 4 Columns.

The Rule: If N=4 Rows are restricted to N=4 Columns, the logic holds.

The Logic: The 4×4 Grid

By now, you recognize the pattern:

X-Wing: 2 Rows locked in 2 Columns.

Swordfish: 3 Rows locked in 3 Columns.

Jellyfish: 4 Rows locked in 4 Columns.

Because these 4 Rows must place their digit into these 4 Columns, they completely "use up" the capacity of those columns. No other row can place that digit in these columns.

The Elimination

Identify: Find 4 Rows where the digit exists only in Columns A, B, C, and D.

Target: Look at the rest of Columns A, B, C, and D.

Action: Eliminate the candidate from every cell in those columns that is not part of your 4 Base Rows.

Theoretical Limits: The Squirmbag (5×5)

You might ask: "Is there a 5×5 fish?" Yes, it is called a Squirmbag. However, remember the rule of Inversion (from Hidden Quads):

If you have a 5×5 Fish in the Rows...

...that means the other 4 Rows form a Jellyfish (or smaller pattern) in the Columns.

Therefore, you never actually need to look for a Squirmbag. You just look for the smaller Fish in the opposite direction.

How to Spot It

Jellyfish are extremely rare in manually constructed puzzles but common in computer-generated ones.

Strategy: Do not hunt for them specifically. Instead, keep your candidates clean. Often, a Jellyfish reveals itself only after you have failed to find X-Wings and Swordfish.

3. Section Summary

The Jellyfish is the final practical step of the "Basic Fish" ladder. It reinforces the power of "Set Theory" in Sudoku: if a set of N lines is restricted to N perpendicular coordinates, the network is closed. Anything outside that network is false. While you won't use this every day, understanding it proves you have mastered the geometry of the grid.
