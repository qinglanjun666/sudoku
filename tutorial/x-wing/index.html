X-Wing

1. What You Will Master

In this section, you will learn the legendary X-Wing. This is the first of the "Fish" patterns (a family of single-digit rectangular formations). You will master the skill of identifying a rectangle formed by a single digit and understanding how this "locked rectangle" allows you to eliminate candidates from the entire length of the grid.

2. Main Content

The Concept

An X-Wing occurs when you are analyzing a single specific number (e.g., '7') and you find the following pattern: There are exactly two Rows where the number '7' appears only twice, and importantly, these appearances align perfectly in the same two Columns.

The Shape: It forms a rectangle. Four cells, creating the corners of a box.

The Logic: Because of this rigid alignment, a logical "X" relationship is formed.

The Logic: Why It Works

Imagine Row 2 and Row 6 both have candidate '7's only in Column 3 and Column 8.

Row 2: Must have a 7 in Col 3 OR Col 8.

Row 6: Must have a 7 in Col 3 OR Col 8.

This creates a linked dependency.

Option A: If Row 2 puts its 7 in Col 3... then Row 6 must put its 7 in Col 8.

Option B: If Row 2 puts its 7 in Col 8... then Row 6 must put its 7 in Col 3.

In either scenario (A or B), Column 3 and Column 8 are permanently occupied by these two rows. The '7's for these columns are provided exclusively by Row 2 and Row 6.

The Elimination

Because Col 3 and Col 8 are "locked" by the X-Wing:

You can look up and down the rest of Column 3.

You can look up and down the rest of Column 8.

Action: Eliminate the candidate '7' from every other cell in those columns (except the four corners of the X-Wing).

Horizontal vs. Vertical X-Wings

The logic works both ways:

Row X-Wing: You find the pattern in Rows (Base), and eliminate in Columns (Cover).

Column X-Wing: You find the pattern in Columns (Base), and eliminate in Rows (Cover).

Rule: If two Columns have a digit only in the same two Rows, you eliminate that digit from the rest of those Rows.

How to Spot It

Scanning for X-Wings requires "Single Digit Filtering."

Highlight all '5's on the board.

Look for rows that have exactly two '5's.

Do those two '5's line up vertically with another row that has exactly two '5's?

If yes, you have an X-Wing.

3. Section Summary

The X-Wing is all about identifying a Closed Rectangular Loop. When two rows limit a number to the same two columns, they effectively claim those columns for themselves. Any other candidate of that number sitting in the "line of fire" (the rest of the column) is invalid. Remember: The X-Wing only works on one number at a time.
