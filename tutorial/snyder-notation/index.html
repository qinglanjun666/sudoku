Snyder Notation

1. What You Will Master

In this section, you will learn Snyder Notation, a clean and efficient way to take notes. Named after former World Sudoku Champion Thomas Snyder, this technique prevents your grid from becoming a messy "soup" of numbers. You will learn the strict rule of when to write a candidate and, more importantly, when not to.

2. Main Content

The Problem with Over-Marking

Beginners often fill every empty cell with every possible number (e.g., writing "1234" in a cell). This is called "candidate soup." It overwhelms your brain and hides the useful patterns.

The Snyder Rule

The rule is simple but strict:

"Only pencil in candidates for a number if it can go in exactly TWO positions within a 3×3 Box."

  • If it can go in 1 spot: Solve it immediately (Hidden Single).
  • If it can go in 2 spots: Mark them.
  • If it can go in 3 or more spots: Do not mark anything. Leave it blank.

Why It Works

  • Speed: You write less, so you play faster.
  • Clarity: When you see pencil marks in a box, you instantly know "It's one of these two."
  • Automatic Solving: If you eliminate one of the two Snyder marks, you instantly know the other one is the solution. You don't have to re-scan.

Example

Look at Box 1. We are scanning for the number 7.

Row 1 is blocked by a 7.

Column 1 is blocked by a 7.

We have two empty cells left in Box 1 that can take a 7.

Action: Write a small '7' in the corner of those two cells.

The "cleanup" Phase

Later in the game, you solve a cell that points to one of your Snyder pairs.

Trigger: You place a big '7' in Row 2.

Reaction: You look at Box 1. One of your '7' marks is now impossible.

Result: The other '7' mark becomes the Real Solution. Fill it in immediately!

3. Section Summary

Snyder Notation is about discipline. By restricting your notes to only "two possibilities per box," you keep the board clean and highlight strong links between cells. This notation style is the foundation for spotting advanced patterns like pairs and triples later on. If you see three or more options, resist the urge to mark them—wait for more information.
