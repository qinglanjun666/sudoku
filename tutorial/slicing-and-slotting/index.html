Slicing and Slotting

1. What You Will Master

In this final section of the Novice Level, you will learn Slicing and Slotting, a variation of cross-hatching designed for speed. You will also learn the concept of "Parking" (or localized candidates)—what to do when cross-hatching almost works but leaves you with two options.

2. Main Content

Slicing (The Setup)

Slicing is the act of rapidly identifying which rows and columns are "claimed" by a specific digit.

Instead of looking at a Box and checking neighbors, you look at two columns containing the number '4'.

You instantly know: "The '4' in the middle box must be in that third vertical strip."

This is a faster mental shortcut than full cross-hatching.

Slotting (The Placement)

Slotting is the act of finding the specific hole for the number once you have sliced the area.

Once you know the '4' must be in the "middle column strip," you check the intersecting rows.

If a row already has a '4', you "slot" the number into the remaining free cell.

Parking (The Intro to Pencil Marks)

Sometimes, after Slicing and Slotting, you are left with two possible cells for a number. You can't solve it yet.

Don't Guess! Beginners often guess here.

Park It: Mentally note (or visualize) that "The 4 is in one of these two spots."

This prepares you for Level 2, where we will start writing these down as "Snyder Notation." For now, just remember the location.

The "Ping-Pong" Effect

Effective scanning involves bouncing your eyes back and forth.

You find a '5' in the top-left.

Your eyes shoot right to see if it affects the top-right.

Your eyes shoot down to see if it affects the bottom-left.

You place a number, and immediately ask: "What did this new number just break?"

3. Section Summary

Slicing and Slotting is about seeing the grid as a series of vertical and horizontal "bands" rather than just individual boxes. It speeds up your game significantly. When you are stuck with two options, remember them but do not guess. You have now graduated from the Novice Level! You are no longer just looking at cells; you are seeing the flow of the numbers across the board.
